# Value Proposition Canvas

A strategic design tool by Alexander Osterwalder that ensures a product's value proposition is explicitly aligned with what the customer actually needs. It consists of two sides: the Customer Profile (what the customer is trying to do, what hurts, what they want) and the Value Map (what the product offers to address those elements). The power of the canvas is in making the fit — or misfit — between the two sides visible.

## Steps

1. **Map Customer Jobs** — List everything the customer is trying to accomplish: functional jobs (tasks to complete), social jobs (how they want to be perceived), and emotional jobs (how they want to feel). Rank by importance to the customer. Include supporting jobs like buying, learning, and switching.

2. **Identify Customer Pains** — Document the negative outcomes, risks, and obstacles the customer experiences before, during, and after trying to get jobs done. Include functional pains (doesn't work, too slow), emotional pains (frustration, anxiety), and ancillary pains (cost, learning curve). Rate each pain by severity.

3. **Identify Customer Gains** — Document the outcomes and benefits the customer desires or would be delighted by. Distinguish between required gains (minimum expectations), expected gains (standard), desired gains (beyond expectations), and unexpected gains (pleasant surprises). Rate each gain by relevance.

4. **Design Pain Relievers** — For each significant customer pain, define how the product specifically eliminates or reduces it. Be concrete: not "easy to use" but "completes the task in 2 clicks instead of 7." Not every pain needs a reliever — focus on the most severe ones.

5. **Design Gain Creators** — For each important customer gain, define how the product produces or enables it. Again, be specific and measurable. Focus on the gains that matter most to the target segment and that differentiate the product from alternatives.

6. **Evaluate Fit** — Assess the alignment between the Customer Profile and the Value Map. Strong fit means the product addresses the most important jobs, relieves the most severe pains, and creates the most relevant gains. Identify gaps (important jobs/pains/gains with no corresponding product feature) and waste (features that address nothing important).

## Output Format

A structured report containing:

- **Customer Profile**:
  - Jobs (ranked by importance, categorized by type)
  - Pains (ranked by severity)
  - Gains (ranked by relevance, categorized by type)
- **Value Map**:
  - Products and Services (the offering)
  - Pain Relievers (mapped to specific pains)
  - Gain Creators (mapped to specific gains)
- **Fit Assessment**: Visual or tabular mapping showing which customer elements are addressed and which are not
- **Gaps**: Important customer jobs, pains, or gains that the current product does not address
- **Waste**: Product features or capabilities that do not map to any important customer element
- **Priority Actions**: Recommended changes to strengthen the fit — features to add, modify, or remove
